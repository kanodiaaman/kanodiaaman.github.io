CC Pins, VDMs, & CEC
Inside the Control Wire of USB‑C

How a single-wire HDMI control bus silently travels through your USB-C cable — and why that matters for your TV, monitor, and every screen in between.

⏱ 5 min read 📐 Technical Overview 🗓 2025

What Is CEC, and Why Do We Even Care?

If you've ever pressed a single button on your TV remote and watched your soundbar, Blu-ray player, and streaming box all power on together — congratulations, you've already used CEC without knowing it.

Consumer Electronics Control (CEC) is a one-wire communication protocol built into every HDMI cable since version 1.0. It lets devices on the same HDMI network talk to each other — sending commands like "play," "pause," "set volume," or "power off" across a shared bus using just a single wire (pin 13 on a standard HDMI connector).

Think of CEC like a walkie-talkie line running alongside the main HDMI highway. The audio and video data race at full speed on their own lanes, while CEC quietly whispers device commands over its one dedicated wire at a leisurely 400 bits/second.

CEC is simple, proven, and powerful. The only problem? HDMI uses a big, bulky Type-A connector. In a world moving to the slim, reversible, all-in-one USB-C connector, we needed a way to bring CEC along for the ride.

Enter USB-C — Not Just a Charging Port

Most people see USB-C as a charging cable. Engineers see it as a reconfigurable, multi-protocol superhighway. Inside a USB-C connector there are 24 pins — and two of them, named CC1 and CC2, are especially interesting.

Fig 1 — USB-C Connector: CC Pin Locations

The CC (Configuration Channel) pins were originally designed for one job: negotiating what kind of connection is being made. When you plug in USB-C, CC pins are the first to speak — they figure out cable orientation, power delivery roles, and whether an alternate mode (like DisplayPort or HDMI) should be activated.

But these clever little pins have a second life: once a connection is established, they also carry the Power Delivery (PD) protocol — and it's inside this PD protocol where CEC has found its new home.

VDMs — The Secret Envelope Inside PD

USB Power Delivery communicates over CC pins using small structured messages. Alongside the standard messages — "I can supply 20V," "I need 5A" — the spec includes a special message type called a Vendor Defined Message (VDM).

Think of a VDM as a blank envelope inside the PD mail system. The USB-C standard defines the envelope format; the contents are entirely up to the sender. Any company can use VDMs to carry custom data over the CC wire.

How CEC Rides Inside a VDM

Here is where it all comes together. When an HDMI Alt Mode connection is active over USB-C, the HDMI specification defines its own set of Structured VDMs to manage the HDMI link — including one dedicated to transporting CEC frames.

The Step-by-Step Journey of a CEC Command

Step 1 — Device Connect. You plug a USB-C cable between a host (say, a laptop) and a display. The CC pins immediately begin talking via PD to negotiate power and capabilities.

Step 2 — HDMI Alt Mode Activation. Through a series of Structured VDMs, both sides agree to enter HDMI Alternate Mode. Some USB-C pins are now reconfigured to carry HDMI video and audio signals.

Step 3 — CEC Channel Established. The HDMI Alt Mode spec reserves a dedicated VDM command type for CEC. The two devices exchange VDMs to set up a logical CEC link over the same CC wire.

Step 4 — CEC Frames Fly. When you press a button on your TV remote to control volume, a CEC frame is generated. It gets encapsulated into a VDM payload, handed to the PD controller, and transmitted as a PD message over the CC wire — arriving at the laptop or display on the other end.

Step 5 — Extraction & Action. The receiving device's PD controller peels off the VDM wrapper, extracts the CEC frame, and passes it up to the application layer — which then adjusts volume, changes input, or powers down, just like it would on a traditional HDMI cable.

In short: CEC doesn't get its own wire in USB-C. Instead, it hitchhikes inside VDM envelopes that travel over CC — the same wire already busy managing power delivery. It's elegant protocol layering at its finest.

Why This Matters — Real-World Impact

This isn't just academic. As docking stations, laptops, and monitors increasingly rely on a single USB-C cable for everything — power, video, audio, and control — CEC-over-VDM is what makes one-cable AV control possible.

The Bigger Picture

The USB-C connector is a masterclass in protocol layering. Its CC pins — just two tiny wires — carry enough information to negotiate power, configure alternate modes, and now tunnel a legacy AV control protocol that was never designed to travel over USB at all.

VDMs are the magic trick: a standardized "custom envelope" mechanism that lets the HDMI ecosystem piggyback CEC signals onto the same physical wire already doing heavy-duty PD negotiation. The result is a cleaner desk, fewer cables, and a smarter, more integrated device ecosystem.

Next time you plug in a single USB-C cable and your monitor lights up, your speakers respond to your keyboard, and your device charges — remember that the humble CC pin is quietly orchestrating all of it, one VDM at a time.
